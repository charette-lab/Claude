# Premise Audit — What Must Be True for the TBK Thesis to Hold

The deck's conclusion chain:
**C1** TBK's margins are structurally too low, not industry-given →
**C2** ~10% operating margin is capturable in 18 months (Phase 1) →
**C3** Peer-level R&D is necessary and affordable inside that P&L →
**C4** Normalized TBK is worth a multiple of today's ~¥9.6bn market cap.

Each conclusion rests on stated premises (in the deck, evidence-tiered) and assumed premises
(unstated but load-bearing). The assumed ones are where the thesis can break.

## ASSUMED premises (unstated, load-bearing)

### On pricing (the biggest value driver)

- **A1 — Volume retention under repricing.** OEMs accept +9% average without resourcing volume,
  accelerating dual-sourcing, or sponsoring a new entrant (SHW's reports show Asian entrants
  exist). Assumes sole-source stickiness (the EC M.8102 finding) transfers to Japan and holds
  for 18+ months. *If even one major program resources, the pricing math inverts.*
- **A2 — The "market price" is real.** The ~30% gap presumes a liquid reference price for truck
  drum brakes/pumps. The observed 2x on non-keiretsu customers (Cummins at 79.8% CM) may partly
  be small-batch premium pricing, not an achievable at-scale market price. Phase 1's +9% is
  robust to a lot of error here; Phase 2's +15% is not.
- **A3 — No clawback.** The relationships that suppressed price don't respond with offsetting
  demands (resumed annual cost-downs, tooling givebacks, payment terms) that quietly reverse
  the captured margin at the next cycle.
- **A12 — Increases stick cumulatively** — competitors don't price down underneath TBK once it
  moves, and renewals don't give back the prior round.

### On the product and its ownership

- **A9 — TBK owns what it would reprice and export.** **RESOLVED — CONFIRMED TRUE**
  (owner statement, 2026-06-12): TBK owns its products/designs (承認図 supplier-owned-drawing
  position). Consequences: the repricing leverage is TBK's own, the SAW performance claim
  attaches to TBK's IP, and the India/export arbitrage is not contractually blocked by drawing
  ownership. Residual diligence is light: document the ownership per product line in the
  day-0-30 contract map (export-license or supply-agreement clauses could still constrain
  specific programs).
- **A7 — R&D converts to sockets.** Spending 4.7% of sales wins ADB and e-pump positions despite
  the 60–130x knowledge-stock gap — i.e., the Brakes India alliance effectively substitutes for
  the missing stock, and Japanese OEMs will qualify TBK's ADB rather than defaulting to Knorr.
  The UD precedent (all cells competitor-held) shows they sometimes don't.

### On execution

- **A4 — Restructuring is executable in Japan.** ~450–1,000 heads reducible by voluntary
  programs at ¥2–3bn without industrial action, without quality slippage at JIT-critical
  plants (OEM consent is a practical requirement for consolidation), and **without losing the
  skilled workers the ADB/e-pump industrialization needs** — the cut and the build compete for
  the same people.
- **A6 — Management capability and will.** The team that produced a decade of negative ROIIC
  executes an operator-grade plan — or is augmented; the board accepts margin-over-volume,
  which contradicts 75 years of keiretsu culture; Brakes India's 10% provides enough governance
  push. The FY3/26 turn (NA exit, capex up, margin +1.9pts) is evidence, but one year.
- **A14 — The data is representative.** H1 FY2025 product P&Ls are normal periods; the share
  map is current; the 70%/90% share figures (analyst-sourced) are directionally right.

### On the environment

- **A5 — No demand cliff in the window.** Japanese truck cycle roughly flat (guidance already
  −3.2%), Thailand (26% of sales) doesn't repeat its credit slump, no FX dislocation (the
  analysis mixes ¥150/USD).
- **A8 — The timing window stays open at both ends.** The diesel/drum melt stays slow enough
  that legacy cash funds the transition (METI/our research: yes, to ~2030), AND the disc flip
  arrives soon enough that ADB investment isn't stranded. The deck assumes both tails away.
- **A11 — Cross-cultural transplant validity.** Concentric and Haldex — Western, aftermarket-
  heavy, contract-law environments — are valid behavioral benchmarks for a keiretsu supplier.
  The margin *arithmetic* transfers; the *bargaining dynamics* may not.

### On the numbers themselves

- **A10 — Translation premises.** "Marginal profit" (TBK), "Contribution I/II" (Concentric,
  Haldex) are treated as comparable after-material measures; JGAAP/IFRS gross margins are
  bridged correctly; the ~2x contribution gap survives definitional differences (it is large
  enough that it plausibly does); tax at 30%; no working-capital drag from growth modeled.

## Which premises carry which conclusion

- **C1 (margins structurally low)** is the most robust: it stands on S1+S2+S6 alone — verified
  filings and internal data — even if every management statement (S3–S5) were discounted.
- **C2 (10% in 18 months)** needs A1, A4, A6 — pricing without volume loss, executable
  restructuring, management will. It is deliberately buffered (only 1/3 of the stated gap), so
  S3 can be one-third wrong without breaking it.
- **C3 (R&D necessary & affordable)** needs S7+S8 (strong) and A7 (genuinely uncertain).
- **C4 (valuation multiple)** needs everything above plus A8 — and is therefore the most
  fragile; it is also the one with the most slack (~2.2x earnings at Phase 1 leaves room for
  several premises to disappoint).

| Premise at risk | Day-X test |
|---|---|
| A1/A2/A3 (pricing reality) | The first two keiretsu renegotiations + the aftermarket +10% move — real price-capture data by day ~120 |
| A9 (drawing/IP ownership) | **RESOLVED — confirmed: TBK owns its products.** Day-0–30 task downgraded to documentation |
| A4 (restructuring) | Voluntary-program take-up rate by day 180 |
| A6 (management) | Whether the quote freeze and stop-loss exits actually execute by day 90 |
| A7 (sockets) | Conversion of the two e-pump development sockets to committed programs by day 90 |
| A5/A8 (environment) | Monitored, not testable — sizing buffers carry them |

**Bottom line (updated):** the descriptive conclusion (C1) holds on verified evidence alone.
With **A9 now confirmed (TBK owns its products)**, the single remaining premise that can kill
the prescriptive thesis is **A1 — volume retention under repricing** — testable by the first
two keiretsu renegotiations (~day 120 of the plan). A9's confirmation also strengthens three
levers directly: SAW repricing (it is TBK's design being underpriced), the India/export
arbitrage (no drawing-ownership block), and the aftermarket expansion.
