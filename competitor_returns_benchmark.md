## What the numbers say

1. **TBK has the worst incremental returns in the entire peer set, at every horizon.**
   ROIIC of −26% (3yr), −10% (7yr), −4.4% (14yr): every dollar reinvested over the past
   decade-plus destroyed value — worse than Akebono, worse than Mikuni. Average ROIC (3.2% over
   7 years, 1.3% over 3) sits far below any cost of capital.
2. **The reinvestment-rate columns expose the worst possible combination.** TBK, Akebono, and
   Mikuni all show RR = 1.00 — full reinvestment of operating profit — *at negative incremental
   returns*. The winners reinvest only 39–54% of profits but earn +10–17% on the increment
   (SAF-Holland: RR 0.39 at ROIIC +13–17% — the most disciplined allocator in the set).
   Capital allocation quality, not capital quantity, separates the two groups.
3. **Reinvestment quality maps almost perfectly onto shareholder returns**: Cummins (+21%/yr over
   7 years), SAF-Holland (+5.0%), Knorr (+3.2%), Brembo (+1.2%) vs Mikuni (−9.6%), Akebono
   (−10.8%), TBK (−6.4%). The market pays for ROIIC.
4. **TBK's balance sheet is the strongest in the group — and the most sterile.** 55.5% equity
   ratio, net debt of effectively zero (0.45x EBITA), no bailout paper (unlike Akebono), no
   leverage overhang (unlike Mikuni at 9.6x, Hanon, ZF at 13.3% equity). But that conservative
   capital sits in invested capital equal to 82% of sales — underloaded factories earning ~0 —
   versus SAF-Holland running the same industry at 18% of sales by being asset-light.
5. **The capital divide mirrors the margin divide.** The same companies that out-margin TBK
   (Knorr, Cummins, SAF-Holland, Concentric) also earn 11–23% on capital with sub-1x leverage
   discipline, and the same Japanese cohort (TBK, Akebono, Mikuni) clusters at 2–4% ROIC with
   RR = 1.00. This is the system-level evidence that the problem is allocation and pricing,
   not industry economics.
6. **Implication for the reinvestment program**: TBK's negative ROIIC record is the strongest
   argument FOR gating the R&D catch-up, not against funding it. The balance sheet can fund the
   program several times over (net cash position, ¥7.3bn cash, untapped debt capacity at 0.45x);
   what must change is what the money buys — e-pump/ADB sockets and footprint repair (positive-
   ROIIC uses) instead of maintaining underutilized capacity (the proven negative-ROIIC use).
   SAF-Holland is the template: reinvest less than half of profits, at high incremental returns,
   and return the rest.

## Caveats

- The dataset's LTM rows have gaps for Brembo/Knorr/SAF-Holland (gross margin, equity, net debt
  blank/zero) — filled from filings and verified aggregators where needed.
- RR = 1.00 for TBK/Akebono/Mikuni likely reflects a framework cap (reinvestment ≥ NOPAT in
  loss/low-profit years); directionally it means nothing was distributed from value creation.
- ROE comparisons are distorted by leverage (Cummins ~21% on 39% equity ratio vs TBK −0.4% on
  55.5%) — ROIC/ROIIC is the cleaner cross-company lens, which is why it leads this review.
- Aisin reports a 13% ROIC target by FY2031 (vs ~5–8% ROE today) — even the giant is being
  pushed to raise capital productivity.
