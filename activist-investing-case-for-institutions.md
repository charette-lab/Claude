# The Case for Activist Investing: Why Endowments and Pension Funds Should Allocate to the Activist Asset Class

*An institutional investment memorandum*

---

## Executive Summary

Activist investing — the strategy of acquiring meaningful equity stakes in
public companies and then working, publicly or privately, to change how those
companies are governed, capitalized, or operated — has matured from an
opportunistic niche into a durable, institutional-grade asset class. For
long-horizon, governance-minded asset owners such as endowments and pension
funds, activism offers a rare combination: equity-like returns, a differentiated
and partially uncorrelated source of alpha, a natural fit with fiduciary
stewardship obligations, and a transparent, fundamentally-driven thesis that
trustees can understand and defend.

This memorandum makes the case across five dimensions: (1) the return and alpha
profile, (2) alignment with the long-term institutional liability horizon and
fiduciary duty, (3) diversification and risk-adjusted contribution to the total
portfolio, (4) the governance and stewardship synergies that distinguish
activism from passive ownership, and (5) a realistic discussion of risks and a
practical framework for implementation. Our conclusion is that a measured
allocation — typically 3% to 8% of the growth or public-equity sleeve — is
warranted for most institutions that can tolerate concentration and a multi-year
holding period.

## Page 1 — The Return and Alpha Proposition

The foundational reason any asset owner adds an asset class is the expectation
of attractive risk-adjusted returns. Activist strategies earn returns through a
specific and repeatable mechanism: identifying companies that trade at a
discount to their intrinsic or achievable value because of correctable
problems — bloated cost structures, undermanaged balance sheets, value-destroying
acquisitions, poor capital allocation, entrenched boards, or conglomerate
discounts — and then acting as the catalyst that closes that gap.

This is a structurally different return source than the broad market beta
captured by an index fund. A passive holder of an underperforming company simply
absorbs that underperformance. An activist *changes the outcome*. Academic and
practitioner research consistently documents this catalyst effect: studies of
activist 13D filings find statistically significant positive abnormal returns in
the window around announcement (commonly cited in the range of 5% to 7% on
average), and — importantly for skeptics who fear short-termism — subsequent
research tracking targets over the following three to five years generally finds
that operating performance and total shareholder returns *improve* rather than
revert. The "value extraction at the expense of the long term" narrative is not
well supported by the longer-horizon data.

For an institution, three features of this return stream stand out:

- **Idiosyncratic alpha.** A meaningful portion of an activist's return comes
  from the specific change effected at a specific company, not from the market
  going up. This is precisely the kind of return that is scarce and valuable in
  a portfolio dominated by beta.
- **A defined catalyst and timeline.** Unlike a generic "deep value" bet that
  may stay cheap indefinitely, activist positions come with an identified path
  and agent of change. This shortens and clarifies the route to value
  realization.
- **Capacity-constrained, skill-dependent returns.** Because activism requires
  concentrated capital, operational expertise, and the willingness to take
  public positions, it is not easily commoditized or arbitraged away — a
  desirable property for an asset owner seeking persistent alpha.

The honest caveat is dispersion: manager selection matters enormously here, more
than in almost any other equity strategy. The gap between top-quartile and
median activist managers is wide. We address selection in Page 5.

## Page 2 — Alignment With the Institutional Horizon and Fiduciary Duty

Endowments and pension funds are among the most natural owners of activist
strategies because their structural characteristics map directly onto what the
strategy requires to succeed.

**Long time horizons.** Activist theses take time to play out — board seats are
won at annual meetings, strategic reviews unfold over quarters, divestitures and
operational turnarounds over years. Investors who can hold through this process
capture the full value; investors forced to mark-to-market and exit on short
notice do not. Perpetual endowments and pension funds with long-dated
liabilities are precisely the patient capital that activism rewards. Where a
hedge fund-of-funds might face quarterly redemption pressure, an endowment can
underwrite a three-year thesis with conviction.

**Fiduciary duty and the prudent steward.** A trustee's fiduciary duty is not
satisfied by passive ownership alone; it is increasingly understood to include
*responsible stewardship* of the assets owned. Allocating to managers who hold
boards accountable, who insist on rational capital allocation, and who push for
governance reforms is a direct expression of the duty to act in beneficiaries'
long-term economic interest. Far from being in tension with fiduciary
obligations, well-governed activism is an instrument of them.

**Mission and reputational alignment.** For many endowments — particularly
university and foundation endowments — the act of demanding accountability and
better governance from portfolio companies is consistent with institutional
values around transparency and good management. Activism lets the institution be
an owner that *votes its conscience and its analysis*, rather than a silent
recipient of whatever management decides.

**Engagement as risk management.** A large passive owner is exposed to the
governance failures of every company it holds, with no lever to pull. An
allocation to activism gives the institution exposure to a strategy whose entire
purpose is to detect and remediate exactly those failures — turning a source of
hidden portfolio risk into a source of return.

## Page 3 — Diversification and the Total-Portfolio View

The strongest portfolio-construction argument for activism is not its standalone
return but its *contribution to the total portfolio* through differentiated
return drivers.

**A distinct return engine.** While activist positions are long equities and
therefore carry equity beta, their excess returns are driven by
company-specific events — proxy contests, settlements, spin-offs, management
changes, capital-return programs. These catalysts are largely independent of the
macro factors (rates, growth, inflation) that drive most of the rest of an
institutional portfolio. Adding a return stream whose alpha component is
event-driven and idiosyncratic improves the portfolio's overall
Sharpe ratio even if the standalone volatility of the sleeve is higher than the
index.

**Diversification within the equity allocation.** Most institutions are heavily
exposed to market-cap-weighted equity beta and, increasingly, to the same
mega-cap growth names through both active and passive vehicles. Activist
managers tend to operate in small- and mid-cap value territory and in special
situations — segments that are underrepresented in a typical institutional book.
The allocation therefore diversifies *style and capitalization* exposure, not
just adds another large-cap manager.

**Crisis behavior and dry powder.** Activism is counter-cyclical in an important
sense: market dislocations create the valuation gaps and management missteps that
generate the next generation of targets. Managers with locked-up, patient capital
can deploy into weakness. For an institution rebalancing through a drawdown, an
activist sleeve can be a source of opportunistic, value-oriented deployment
rather than forced selling.

**Lower fee drag than many alternatives.** Relative to other alpha-seeking
alternatives — private equity, venture, certain hedge fund strategies — activist
equity offers daily-priced, publicly-traded underlying exposure (even when
accessed through a fund vehicle), greater transparency into the holdings and
thesis, and frequently more favorable liquidity terms. The institution is not
locked into a ten-year blind pool to access the strategy.

A simple framing for trustees: activism is a way to harvest some of the
return-enhancing properties of private-equity-style operational engagement —
buying underperformers and fixing them — but within the public markets, with
better liquidity, lower fees, and full price transparency.

## Page 4 — Governance, Stewardship, and the ESG Synergy

The past decade has reframed activism. What was once caricatured as
corporate-raider opportunism is now widely understood as one of the most
effective real-world mechanisms for improving corporate governance — and that
shift creates a powerful synergy for institutional owners with stewardship
mandates.

**Activism as applied governance.** The core activist toolkit — challenging
entrenched boards, insisting on independent directors, linking executive pay to
performance, ending value-destroying empire-building, demanding capital
discipline — is, line for line, the governance "G" of ESG put into practice.
Where a passive owner can only vote on management's proposals, an activist
*originates* the proposals that improve governance. For a pension or endowment
that has committed to responsible-investment principles, an activist allocation
is among the most direct ways to make those principles consequential rather than
declarative.

**The rise of "constructive" and ESG-oriented activism.** A growing cohort of
managers pursues change through engagement rather than confrontation, and an
increasing number target environmental and social improvements where those
improvements are tied to long-term enterprise value — energy transition capital
plans, supply-chain risk, human-capital management. This lets an institution
express both a return objective and a stewardship objective through the same
allocation, with the discipline that the thesis must still be grounded in value
creation.

**Accountability that compounds across the whole portfolio.** Even modest
activist positions can produce governance improvements — say-on-pay reform,
board refreshment norms, capital-return discipline — that influence peer
companies and set precedents. An institution that funds credible activists is, in
effect, helping to raise governance standards across the market it is broadly
exposed to through its passive holdings. There is a positive externality: the
activist sleeve can improve the quality of the beta the rest of the portfolio
owns.

**Defensibility to stakeholders.** Activism gives an institution a clear,
articulable story for its beneficiaries, faculty, retirees, or board: *we own
companies, and we use that ownership to make them better-run and more valuable.*
That narrative is far easier to defend than opaque alternative strategies, and it
turns the institution's scale into a force for accountability.

## Page 5 — Risks, Manager Selection, and Implementation

A credible case must address the risks candidly. Activism is not a free lunch,
and the dispersion of outcomes is the central challenge.

**Key risks**

- **Concentration risk.** Activist portfolios are deliberately concentrated;
  a handful of positions drive returns. This raises single-name risk and
  volatility relative to a diversified index.
- **Headline and litigation risk.** Public campaigns can become contentious,
  drawing media attention and occasionally litigation. Institutions must be
  comfortable being associated, indirectly, with public confrontations.
- **Liquidity during a campaign.** Large stakes in smaller companies can be
  costly to exit quickly, and managers may impose lock-ups to protect the
  thesis. Liquidity terms must be matched to the institution's needs.
- **Manager dispersion.** As noted, the gap between skilled and unskilled
  activists is unusually wide. A poorly chosen manager can destroy value or
  pursue campaigns for the sake of fee-generating activity.
- **Crowding and capacity.** Popular targets can attract multiple activists,
  compressing returns; the best managers actively manage their capacity.

**Manager selection criteria**

Given that dispersion, due diligence should weight: a demonstrated, repeatable
*process* for sourcing and underwriting targets; genuine operational and
governance expertise (not just financial engineering); a track record of
*settlements and outcomes*, not merely splashy launches; appropriate fund size
relative to the opportunity set; alignment of fee terms with long-term value
creation; and references from companies and other directors, not only investors.

**Implementation framework**

1. **Size the allocation prudently.** A 3%–8% allocation within the growth or
   public-equity sleeve captures meaningful diversification and alpha without
   overexposing the portfolio to concentration risk.
2. **Diversify across managers and campaign styles.** Blend confrontational and
   constructive/engagement managers, and across sectors, to smooth the
   event-timing of returns.
3. **Match liquidity terms to the liability profile.** Use the institution's
   long horizon as an advantage, but ensure lock-ups are understood and modeled
   in the liquidity budget.
4. **Govern the allocation explicitly.** Establish clear board-level guidelines
   on reputational tolerance, ESG alignment, and reporting expectations so the
   allocation is defensible and well-understood by trustees.
5. **Benchmark thoughtfully.** Evaluate against a public-equity benchmark on a
   multi-year basis, recognizing that activist returns are lumpy and
   event-driven; short-term tracking error is expected and acceptable.

## Conclusion

For an endowment or pension fund, the activist asset class is uniquely
well-matched to institutional strengths: a long horizon that rewards patience, a
fiduciary duty that is *expressed* rather than compromised by engaged ownership,
and a return stream whose alpha is idiosyncratic, event-driven, and diversifying.
It offers private-equity-style operational upside within the liquidity and
transparency of public markets, and it converts the governance risk embedded in
broad equity ownership into a source of return and stewardship impact.

The risks — concentration, headline exposure, and above all manager dispersion —
are real and demand disciplined selection and sizing. But they are manageable
within a deliberate framework. For institutions willing to do that work, a
measured allocation to activism is not a speculative bet on confrontation; it is
a prudent, mission-aligned investment in better-run companies and a stronger
total portfolio.
