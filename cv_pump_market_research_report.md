# The Commercial Vehicle Pump Market: Development, Structure, and Outlook

**A research review prioritizing peer-reviewed and adversarial primary sources over company promotion**
Prepared 2026-06-12 · Context: analysis of TBK Co., Ltd. (TYO: 7277), whose pump business (truck
water/oil pumps) serves Isuzu, Hino, and Mitsubishi Fuso · Companion to the drum-to-disc brake study

---

## Methodology and limitations

Five parallel research passes (technical evolution, regulatory drivers, electrification economics,
market structure, Japan/Asia) followed by adversarial verification of 25 load-bearing claims by
three independent voters: **25/25 supported**, several with material caveats noted in place. Per
the brief, company self-promotion was de-prioritized; where company information is used it comes
preferentially from **adversarial documents** — securities-report risk factors, antitrust
decisions, takeover documents, impairments, and divestment records. Full-text retrieval was
blocked in this environment, so claims rest on abstracts, regulatory text excerpts, and
multi-source verification. One circulating claim was found to be **erroneous**: a supposed
TBK–Mikuni thermal-management alliance (likely a conflation with Mikuni's agreement with Suzuki).

Confidence key: ★★★ peer-reviewed or primary regulatory/legal document, verified;
★★ government/financial-disclosure data, verified by multiple sources; ★ single-source or
estimate, labeled.

## 1. Technical evolution: a small lever on the diesel, refined for decades

The mechanical, engine-driven water and oil pump was never a large efficiency target. The
peer-reviewed energy-balance literature puts pump/accessory parasitics at **~1.3% of fuel energy**
in a heavy-duty diesel (MIT thesis 2012), with NACFE bounding water pumps at **1.1–1.8% of Class 8
fuel use** ★★★/★★. Consequences, all verified:

- **Electrifying the coolant pump on a 13-litre truck engine saves only 0.71–0.94% of fuel**
  (Granitz et al., SN Applied Sciences 2021 — the most rigorous truck-specific study), and the
  saving *shrinks at high load* — which is why long-haul OEMs adopted variable and clutched
  mechanical pumps before full electrification, and why urban/bus duty cycles electrify first ★★★.
- **Variable-displacement oil pumps deliver ~1–1.5% in peer-reviewed tests** (SAE 2012-01-0408 —
  caveat: measured on a small gasoline engine, including a piston-cooling-jet contribution)
  versus **3–6% in industry marketing** — a documented promotion-vs-evidence gap ★★★.
- On-demand pumps nonetheless appear in every high-efficiency demonstration: Cummins' SuperTruck 2
  50% brake-thermal-efficiency engine (55% with waste-heat recovery) explicitly includes a
  variable lube-oil pump and variable cooling ★★★.

The technical story of the ICE era is therefore *incremental refinement of a cheap commodity*:
real but small efficiency value, captured mostly by smarter mechanical pumps. That is the world
TBK's 70-year pump franchise was built for.

## 2. Regulation: asymmetric signals, and Japan sends none

- **US GHG Phase 2 is the only regime with explicit, codified credits for electrified pumps**:
  40 CFR 1037.520 grants 0.5–1 g CO2/ton-mile for electric steering pumps and 0.5–1 g for
  high-efficiency/electric A/C compressors in the GEM compliance model ★★★ (verified against eCFR).
- **The EU's VECTO tool monetizes pump technology by declared class** (fixed / fixed+electronic /
  dual / variable displacement); JRC analysis of declared data found **62.5% of EU heavy vehicles
  still on fixed-displacement steering pumps** — quantified headroom that regulation converts to
  pump-upgrade demand ★★★. The 2024 CO2 targets (−45% by 2030, −65% by 2035, −90% by 2040) shift
  the driver from better diesel pumps to full e-pump architectures on zero-emission trucks ★★★.
- **EPA Phase 3** projects ZEV shares of ~25% (sleeper tractors) to ~60% (light-heavy vocational)
  by MY2032 — pump *replacement*, not refinement ★★★.
- **Japan's heavy-vehicle fuel-economy regime gives essentially no accessory-level signal**: the
  MY2025 standards (+13.4% for trucks) use an engine-fuel-map plus simulation method whose
  documented inputs do not credit pump technology (inferred from method descriptions; no
  contradicting evidence found) ★★. **TBK's home regulatory environment has thus exerted the least
  pressure to advance pump technology** — protective short-term, atrophying long-term.
- Euro 7's cold-start/low-load focus adds an emissions-side driver for electronically controlled
  coolant flow (faster catalyst warm-up) from 2028-29 in heavy duty ★★.

## 3. Electrification: the pump business gets bigger, not smaller — but different

This is the central finding, and it cuts against the intuition that EVs kill pump demand:

- **BEV trucks need multiple separate coolant loops** — battery coolant must stay below ~40°C
  while motor/power-electronics loops run far hotter, so they cannot share a circuit. A diesel
  truck carries one mechanical water pump and one oil pump; a BEV truck typically carries 2–4
  electric coolant pumps plus e-oil pumps for axle/motor lubrication, valves, and chillers ★★★.
- **Fuel-cell trucks are the most pump-intensive vehicles ever built**: 40–45% of fuel energy
  rejected as heat through the coolant, heat-exchanger capacity 1.5–2x a comparable diesel
  (some estimates 3–4x effective), coolant flows an order of magnitude above passenger-car
  loops ★★★ (Energies 2023/2026, Sustainability 2023).
- **Value per unit multiplies**: triangulating Rheinmetall's disclosed order values (€26M for a
  "low six-figure" quantity of 800V pumps), a high-voltage e-pump carries roughly **4–8x the value
  of a mechanical water pump**; times 2–4x more units per vehicle, pump-related content per
  zero-emission truck is plausibly **8–30x a diesel's water-pump value** ★ (inference from vendor
  figures, labeled as such). Pierburg's mid-six-figure-unit order from a North American truck OEM
  for BEV trucks (2028–35) confirms multiple pumps per vehicle at scale ★★.
- Syndicated forecasts put electric-pump market growth at ~12–15% CAGR ★ (methodology opaque;
  directionally consistent with content expansion).

**The catch:** the new sockets are electromechanical (brushless motors, inverters, software,
high-voltage safety) — a different engineering discipline from cast-iron impeller pumps, and the
early CV wins are going to passenger-car-lineage and electronics-capable suppliers, not truck-pump
incumbents.

## 4. Market structure: the adversarial documents tell one story

Assembled per the brief from what companies *do* and what regulators *find*, not what marketing
says — all independently verified ★★★/★★:

- **Rheinmetall sold Pierburg (~€2.0bn revenue, 6,250 employees) to AEQUITA for a provisional
  €350M — ~0.18x sales** — after classifying it as a discontinued operation (Dec 2025) and booking
  ~€350M + ~€200M of impairments. The stated rationale is defense focus; the valuation and
  impairments are the real verdict on ICE-pump economics.
- **A.P. Møller Holding took Concentric private** (SEK 230/share, 61.5% premium, ~15.5x EV/EBITDA,
  delisted Nov 2024) at a cyclical trough — mid-offer, Concentric issued a negative earnings
  update citing reduced orders and restructuring. Patient capital buying the energy-transition
  thesis cheaply while public markets de-rate the sector.
- **SHW** (Pierer Industrie ~92%, de-facto delisted, squeeze-out dynamics) cites in its own
  reports intense competition "particularly from new market participants from Asia" and constant
  customer price-reduction pressure.
- **Hanon Systems** required a takeover (Hankook, 54.8%) and an accounting cleanup — narrowing
  R&D capitalization at ~KRW 21bn cost in Q1 2025 alone, i.e., prior profitability was flattered.
- **Mahle** cut ~1,000 further jobs (R&D-heavy) in Nov 2025 with Engine Systems sales −8%/yr.
  (Caveat from verification: the widely-quoted "ICE no longer a reliable economic driver" line
  could not be sourced and conflicts with Mahle's own ICE-positive messaging — treat as paraphrase.)
- **Dayco's PE owner divested the entire OE engine-driven (Propulsion Solutions) business** to
  AEQUITA (2024) to focus on aftermarket — a revealed-preference exit from OE ICE components.
- The only regulator-vetted concentration data found — **EC case M.8102 (Valeo/FTE, 2017)** —
  documents hydraulic-component niches at **90–100% three-firm concentration** with high switching
  barriers, cleared only with a divestment remedy. These markets are structurally concentrated
  oligopolies; share, once lost, is rarely regained.

**The pattern:** ICE-linked pump assets are leaving public markets at distressed multiples, bought
by private/patient capital; incumbents blame Asian entrants and price-downs; accounting cleanups
reveal flattered profitability. Meanwhile the *electric* pump franchise commands premium strategic
interest (Concentric's 61.5% premium; Pierburg's HV order book as a sale asset).

## 5. Japan: protected, concentrated, and admitting the threat in writing

- **TBK's own securities report says it plainly** ★★ (verified from reproduced filing text):
  *"In commercial vehicles too, buses and small/medium trucks are moving toward EVs; demand for
  our pump products may decline and affect results"* — alongside the parallel disc-brake risk
  admission. The company's own risk factors identify exactly the segments (buses, small/medium
  trucks) where Japanese electrification starts.
- **Provenance caveat on the famous share figures**: TBK's "~70% water pumps / ~90% oil pumps"
  domestic truck share traces only to analyst/secondary sources (note.com, aggregators) — no
  primary filing or shikiho text carries it; shikiho says only "No.1 in truck/bus brakes" ★.
  Directionally credible, but un-audited.
- **TBK is not standing still**: its own product pages document 12/24/48V electric water/oil pumps
  up to ~500W for large HEV/EV/FCV trucks and a thermal-management system with an in-house
  Thermal Control Unit ★★ — a real, if small, response in the right direction.
- **The new-socket competition has already landed**: Mikuni began producing e-oil pumps for an
  *unnamed* domestic small commercial BEV in June 2022 (the eCanter attribution circulating in
  trade press is unconfirmed — the disclosure names no OEM) ★★; Yamada's e-water pumps ship in
  Honda's light commercial EV ★★★ (company primary). Both are passenger-lineage suppliers crossing
  into commercial e-pumps.
- **Timing cushion**: Japanese electric truck volumes are tiny (Hino Dutro Z EV: ~400 registrations
  in its first year; heavy-truck targets only ~5,000 cumulative units in the 2020s; Hino explicitly
  multi-pathway) ★★ — the mechanical pump franchise erodes slowly in heavy duty. METI-commissioned
  studies frame ~40% of ~30,000 parts (engine/transmission/fuel) as unnecessary in a full BEV ★
  (industry estimate repeated in METI documents), and METI runs dedicated support programs for
  engine-parts suppliers — the Japanese state itself treats TBK's cohort as at-risk.
- No solid evidence that Aisin/Denso are targeting heavy-truck pumps specifically (Aisin touches
  medium-truck water pumps in aftermarket) — the giants' absence leaves the CV e-pump niche
  winnable ★★.

## 6. Synthesis: what the evidence means for a truck-pump incumbent

1. **The old franchise dies slowly, the new one is being allocated now.** Heavy-duty diesel
   volumes — and TBK's mechanical pump annuity — persist well past 2030 in Japan. But the
   evidence from every region is that e-pump sockets are awarded years before volumes ramp
   (Pierburg's 2028–35 order book), and they are sticky oligopoly positions (EC M.8102).
2. **Electrification multiplies pump content 8–30x per vehicle** — the rare transition where the
   incumbent's product category *grows*. The threat is not demand destruction but **socket
   reallocation** to electromechanically capable suppliers (Mikuni, Yamada in Japan; Pierburg,
   Concentric, Hanon globally).
3. **Japan's regulatory vacuum cuts both ways**: no accessory credits means no home-market
   subsidy for pump innovation — TBK's e-pump program must be justified by OEM sockets
   (Isuzu/Fuso/Hino BEV programs, bus electrification), not compliance value.
4. **The endgame for undifferentiated ICE-pump makers is written in the deal record**: 0.18x
   sales (Pierburg), squeeze-outs (SHW), PE carve-outs (Dayco). The winners' path is the
   Concentric model — concentrate on CV niches, convert to e-pumps/thermal systems early, and
   command strategic value (61.5% premium) even in a downturn.
5. **TBK's position, stated without promotion**: a dominant but un-audited domestic share in a
   slowly melting product, a self-admitted EV risk in its filings, a real but sub-scale e-pump/TCU
   response, and a home market whose first electrified segments (buses, small/medium trucks) are
   exactly where its risk factors point. The R&D benchmark from the companion study (pump peers
   at 4–5.5% of sales vs TBK's 2.2%) gives the scale of response the evidence calls for.
