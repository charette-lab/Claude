# The Drum-to-Disc Brake Transition in Commercial Vehicles: Industry Impact

**A research review prioritizing peer-reviewed sources, with industry data clearly labeled**
Prepared 2026-06-12 · Context: analysis of TBK Co., Ltd. (TYO: 7277), a Japanese drum-brake-centric supplier to Isuzu, Mitsubishi Fuso, Hino, and Komatsu

---

## Methodology and honest limitations

Five parallel research passes (regulatory drivers, regional adoption, supplier economics, fleet
total cost of ownership, electrification) followed by adversarial verification of ~25 load-bearing
claims by three independent verification passes. **Network restrictions prevented full-text
retrieval in this environment**, so claims rest on abstracts, search excerpts, and verification
cross-checks rather than full-text reads; paywalled SAE papers are cited at abstract level.
Two claims from the initial search were **refuted or downgraded in verification** and are
corrected below (Euro 7 heavy-duty brake-limit dates; TBK's "70% brake share").

**State of the literature:** engineering and safety-economics literature on drum vs disc in heavy
vehicles is solid (SAE papers since 1990, NHTSA test programs, one 2023 journal TCO study).
However, there is **essentially no peer-reviewed industrial-economics research on how the
transition redistributed profits among brake suppliers** — that story must be assembled from
regulatory filings, merger records, and trade data, and is labeled accordingly throughout.

Confidence key: ★★★ = peer-reviewed or primary regulatory/legal document, verified;
★★ = government/industry data, verified by multiple independent sources; ★ = single-source or
unverified industry estimate.

## 1. Technical and regulatory drivers

**The technical case for discs is long-established in the peer-reviewed literature.** ★★★
Göhring & von Glasner (Daimler-Benz), SAE 902206 (1990), showed on dynamometer and field tests
that heavy-vehicle disc brakes exhibit little performance loss under high thermal load, unlike
drums. The underlying physics was established by Day, Harding & Newcomb (Proc IMechE, 1984):
drum thermal expansion moves the friction surface *away* from the shoes while a disc expands
*toward* the pads, so drums fade and discs don't. Notably, the same 1990 SAE session contains
Kajiyama et al. (Isuzu Motors / Tokyo Buhin Kogyo — i.e., TBK), SAE 902200, "Disc Brakes for
Heavy Duty Trucks in Japan" — TBK has been publishing on CV disc brakes since 1990.

**US regulation pushed performance, not technology.** ★★★ NHTSA's 2009 FMVSS 121 final rule cut
the required 60-mph loaded stopping distance 30% (355 ft → 250 ft), phased in August 2011/2013.
Crucially, NHTSA's own regulatory impact analysis estimated compliance at **$211 per tractor with
enhanced drum brakes versus $1,475 with all-position air discs**, and assumed ~82% of tractors
would comply with drums. This is the single best documented explanation for why US disc adoption
was gradual and market-driven rather than a step change.

**Quantified performance gap.** ★★★ NHTSA's driving-simulator study (DOT HS 811 367, 2010;
108 CDL drivers) found stopping distance fell 17% with enhanced S-cam drums and **30% with air
disc brakes** versus standard drums. The peer-reviewed Ampadu, Alrejjal & Ksaibati (2023) TruckSim
study puts the disc advantage at 10–20%, growing with speed and downgrade.

**Europe regulated fade, and EBS sealed the outcome.** ★★ UNECE R13's Type-II downhill
fade/endurance tests structurally favor thermally stable foundation brakes, and the 1996
Mercedes-Benz Actros — the first series heavy truck with electronically controlled (EBS) disc
brakes on all axles (Daimler's claim; no rival claimant found) — set the European template.
Electro-pneumatic braking, stability control, and now AEB mandates all favor the faster, more
precise actuation of disc + EBS architectures.

## 2. Adoption: three regions, three decades apart

| Region | Status | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Europe | Converted in one wave late 1990s; ~70% EBS/ADB take rate by 2000; near-universal on-highway since mid-2000s; drums ~18% of EU brake demand (off-road/export niches) | ★★ (traces to Haldex's account, widely republished) |
| North America | Tractors: <5% (2011) → ~25% (2018) → ~50% (2023–24); trailers lag at ~12–15% (2022–23) | ★★ (multiple independent trade sources) |
| Japan / Asia-Pacific | Drums remain the **majority** on medium/heavy trucks; specific figures (e.g., "85–90%") could **not** be verified and conflict across market reports | ★ |

Japan's specifics matter for TBK ★★: UD Trucks' Quon (2004) was the Japanese early adopter
(all-new 2017 Quon made all-axle discs standard, billed as a first for Japan); Isuzu fitted
standard all-axle discs (Meritor, with EBS) on its **Australian-market** MY25 FX/FY heavies under
Australian design rules, and the new Quon-based Giga tractor has all-wheel discs — but
Japanese-market Isuzu/Fuso/Hino medium and heavy rigids remain predominantly drum-braked.
Trailers lag everywhere outside Europe for hard economic reasons: upfront cost, ~100 lb/axle
weight (payload), low per-trailer utilization, and unproven resale value.

## 3. Fleet economics (TCO)

- **Hardware premium:** drums cost ~45.7% of discs (peer-reviewed input, Ampadu et al. 2023 ★★★);
  trade consensus ~$300–600 per wheel position, $2,500–5,500 per vehicle ★★.
- **Payback is labor- and uptime-driven:** ~15 min disc pad change vs ~1 hour per wheel-end drum
  reline (ATA TMC figure, ~4:1) ★★; brake inspections drop from ~25 min to ~2 min; drum
  out-of-service violations (adjustment-related, historically approaching 20% of inspections)
  largely disappear ★. Vendor-claimed payback 12–18 months; trade consensus TCO-positive in years
  2–3 for on-highway duty ★ (supplier-influenced).
- **Friction life inverts the aftermarket:** linehaul disc pads run 400k–600k miles and rotors
  700k–1M miles — many tractors now complete a trade cycle with zero or one brake job, versus
  ~2 drum relines ★ (Bendix-sourced).
- **Safety economics:** Ampadu et al. estimate ≥$649M/yr US societal savings ★★★; AAA Foundation
  (2017) estimates universal ADB fitment would prevent up to 2,411 crashes and 37 deaths per year
  ★★; FMCSA values a fatal large-truck crash at $15.2M (2023 dollars) ★★.

## 4. Impact on the supplier industry

**No peer-reviewed study quantifies this** — the following is assembled from primary deal records
and regulatory filings, all independently verified ★★★:

- **Total consolidation of the independents, 2002–2023:** Knorr-Bremse took full control of
  Bendix CVS (2002); ZF acquired Wabco for ~$7.0B (May 2020); Cummins acquired Meritor for ~$3.7B
  (Aug 2022); SAF-Holland acquired Haldex (2022).
- **Antitrust as proof of what became scarce:** the European Commission's 2017 Phase II probe of
  Knorr-Bremse/Haldex cited concentration concerns specifically in **air disc brakes for trucks
  and trailers** and EBS — direct regulatory evidence that ADB technology had become the strategic
  asset in CV braking. The bid collapsed; Haldex ultimately sold to SAF-Holland in 2022 at
  SEK 66/share, roughly **half** Knorr-Bremse's 2017 offer of SEK 125 — a measurable marker of how
  a sub-scale disc player's standalone value eroded.
- **Winners run installed-base annuities:** Knorr-Bremse (~20M disc brakes in operation; aftermarket
  ~28% of its CVS revenue) and ZF/Wabco together hold roughly 39% of global air-brake system sales ★.
  Bendix ADBs became standard on Peterbilt/Kenworth steer axles in 2013, with Freightliner's
  Cascadia following (~2024).
- **The aftermarket structurally shrinks per vehicle:** disc adoption converts a high-frequency,
  labor-heavy reline economy (shoes, linings, drums — the classic revenue base of drum/friction
  specialists) into infrequent, higher-content pad/rotor events. No public source cleanly
  quantifies the industry-level reline-vs-pad revenue split — a genuine data gap ★.
- **Pressure on drum-centric and Japanese suppliers** ★★: Meritor's foundation-brake business
  ended up inside Cummins; Akebono restructured severely 2019–20 (~3,000 jobs, ¥20B bailout —
  though driven mainly by its passenger-car/GM exposure, not CV discs).

### TBK specifically (verified against Japanese filings ★★)

- Brake segment revenue ¥19,098M, +0.3% YoY — flat; customer concentration: Isuzu ¥10,237M,
  Mitsubishi Fuso ¥4,650M, Hino ¥1,757M (Komatsu figure unconfirmed).
- **Correction from verification:** the often-repeated "~70% share" figure could not be confirmed
  for brakes — it appears to belong to TBK's **water pumps** (~90% for oil pumps); for CV brakes
  sources say only "top share" in Japan.
- TBK is not disc-absent: it co-authored Japanese heavy-truck disc work as far back as SAE 902200
  (1990), and exhibited self-developed 17.5/19.5/22.5-inch **full air disc brakes** at the JSAE
  Automotive Engineering Exposition 2022.
- December 2025: **Brakes India (TVS Group) took a 10% stake** in TBK via a capital and business
  alliance covering CV braking portfolios and export markets — scale-seeking via alliance rather
  than head-on competition with Knorr-Bremse/ZF.

## 5. Electrification and the future of friction

- **Regen slashes friction-brake work — peer-reviewed and consistent** ★★★: Zhang et al.
  (J. Hazardous Materials, 2024): −75–87% brake-wear PM2.5, −90–95% PM10; Hagino (JARI,
  *Atmosphere* 2024): −85% PM10, −78% PM2.5, −87% particle number on a PHEV coordination system;
  Environmental Pollution (2023): wear reduction ~64–95% across conditions, scaling roughly
  linearly with the friction-work share regen displaces. Caveat: almost all of this is light-duty;
  heavy-duty quantification (incl. JARI work on Japanese truck cycles) is only now emerging.
  Claims of 3–5x pad life on electric buses trace to a single circular source — direction right,
  multiplier unverified ★.
- **Euro 7 — corrected:** Regulation (EU) 2024/1257 sets the first brake-particle limits at
  7 mg/km PM10 for cars (3 mg/km for pure EVs) ★★★, but **heavy-duty brake-particle limits are
  not yet defined**: per ICCT analysis they are expected to apply only from ~2030, after a
  Commission methodology report due end-2027. (The 2028/29 dates sometimes attached to this are
  Euro 7's heavy-duty *exhaust* dates.)
- **Disc vs drum under electrification is genuinely unresolved.** In passenger EVs, regen has
  *revived* drums: Continental's sealed drum on the VW ID.3/ID.4 rear axle was chosen because,
  when regen does most braking, **corrosion from disuse — not wear — becomes the failure mode**,
  and sealed drums resist it ★★★ (Continental's own engineering rationale). For commercial
  vehicles, the precision-blending demands of regen + EBS and emissions measurability still tilt
  toward discs, but no peer-reviewed CV-specific verdict exists ★.
- **Aftermarket headwind:** global brake-friction market growth projected at only ~2% CAGR with
  electrification an explicit structural drag; value shifts from wear life toward corrosion
  resistance, NVH, and low-dust formulations ★ (market research, methodology opaque).

## 6. Synthesis: what this means for a drum-centric Japanese supplier

1. **The protected home market is the core asset and the core risk.** Japan is the last major
   drum-dominated heavy-truck market, and TBK's revenue is concentrated in OEMs that still spec
   drums domestically. But the perimeter is visibly eroding: UD all-disc since 2017, the new
   Giga tractor all-disc, Isuzu's export heavies all-disc under foreign regulation. History
   (Europe ~5 years, North America ~12 years from inflection to majority) says when Japanese OEMs
   flip, the OE transition can be fast.
2. **The aftermarket cushion thins twice.** Disc adoption cuts friction events per vehicle, and
   electrification (regen) cuts friction work per event — peer-reviewed evidence supports 60–95%
   wear reduction. A linings/reline-centric aftermarket is the most exposed part of the franchise.
3. **The consolidation record is unforgiving to sub-scale brake independents** — every one was
   absorbed 2002–2022, and Haldex's halved exit price shows the cost of waiting. TBK's Brakes
   India alliance (Dec 2025) reads as the textbook response: gain scale and export reach without
   competing head-on with the two systems duopolists.
4. **Watch-items:** Japanese OEM disc standardization announcements (especially Isuzu domestic),
   the EU's heavy-duty brake-particle implementing acts (~2027 report, ~2030 application),
   TBK's air-disc commercialization (the 2022 JSAE prototypes), and the regen-driven shift of
   brake value from wear parts to corrosion/NVH-optimized hardware.
